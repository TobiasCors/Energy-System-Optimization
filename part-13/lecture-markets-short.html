Electricity Markets

Introduction

Electricity markets are complex systems that balance instantaneous supply and demand in a high-stakes economic and physical environment. Understanding how they work is essential for energy professionals.

Key Topics:

  • Market evolution and architecture
  • Price formation mechanisms
  • Renewable integration challenges
  • Current market design debates

Market Fundamentals

Historical Evolution

Electricity markets evolved from vertically integrated monopolies toward liberalized, competitive markets.

Key Changes:

  • Before: Vertically integrated utilities controlled generation, transmission, distribution, and retail
  • Now: Markets separate these functions (unbundling) to foster competition
  • Result: Improved efficiency, lower prices, and innovation

Market Transition

  • Unbundling: Separation of functions to enable competition
  • Marginal Pricing: Prices based on the last unit needed to meet demand (merit order)
  • Complexity: Introduced due to electricity’s unique characteristics

Key Participants

  • Generators: Power plants, renewable energy producers
  • Transmission System Operators (TSOs): Manage high-voltage networks
  • Distribution System Operators (DSOs): Deliver power to end users
  • Suppliers/Retailers: Buy wholesale, sell to consumers
  • Market Operators: Facilitate trading (e.g., EPEX SPOT)
  • Regulators: Ensure fair competition and system reliability

Market Architecture

Market Layers and Time Horizons

Electricity markets comprise several interlinked layers:

  • Forward and futures markets: Price signals months to years ahead, risk hedging
  • Day-ahead markets: Central for scheduling generation against forecast demand
  • Intraday markets: Continuous adjustments for updated forecasts and unexpected events
  • Balancing markets: TSOs manage real-time supply-demand imbalances and grid stability
  • Ancillary service markets: Specialized services (frequency regulation, reserves, voltage support)

Interconnected System

Each market layer serves distinct functions but is interconnected; inefficiencies or changes in one impact others.

Example: The intraday market gains significance as renewable forecast errors grow.

Price Formation

Merit Order & Marginal Cost Principle

Prices in wholesale electricity markets are determined by the marginal cost of the most expensive unit dispatched to meet demand.

How Merit Order Works

  1. Ranking: Power plants bid their ma rginal costs (fuel + operating costs)
  2. Stacking: Plants are arranged from cheapest to most expensive
  3. Selection: Starting with the cheapest, plants are activated until demand is met
  4. Price Setting: The most expensive activated plant sets the market price for ALL generators

Typical Merit Order (€/MWh)

  1. Renewables: €0-5 (no fuel costs)
  2. Nuclear: €10-15 (low operating costs)
  3. Lignite/Coal: €20-40 (fuel costs)
  4. Natural Gas: €40-80 (higher fuel costs)
  5. Oil/Peak Plants: €80-200+ (emergency use)

Merit Order Effect: Renewables push expensive plants out of the market, reducing overall prices.

Key Aspects:

  • Storability problems: Electricity can be stored but not cost-effectively in large quantities, hence must be balanced instantaneously; price volatility can be extreme
  • Merit order effect of renewables: Low marginal cost renewables enter first, depressing prices during high output but creating steep price ramps in scarcity
  • Price spikes: Occur when demand exceeds low-cost supply, triggering peaker plants

Renewable Integration

Impact on Market Dynamics

With rising shares of wind and solar (30-60% or higher in some markets), markets face:

  • Increased price volatility and negative prices during oversupply
  • Reduced running hours and revenues for conventional generators, affecting their economic viability: Missing money problem (investment signal issue but critical for system reliability, increasing renewables reduce the frequency and certainty of high prices needed to justify capital expenditure)
  • Need for flexibility: Demand response, storage, interconnectors become essential

Market Adaptations

Market designs are adjusting by:

  • Enhancing intraday, day-ahead and balancing markets for finer temporal granularity (15-minute resolution new standard since 30th September 2025 in Europe)
  • Implementing forecast improvements and more dynamic dispatch
  • Exploring new products that value flexibility and fast response

Market Design Challenges

European Energy Crisis

The recent Europe-wide energy crisis—gas price surges, nuclear outages, droughts reducing hydro—unveiled vulnerabilities:

  • Marginal pricing exposed volatile swings from ~40 €/MWh average to peaks over 500 €/MWh
  • Renewable generators earned “windfall profits” when they received high prices during scarcity periods despite low costs

Critical Debates

Is marginal pricing still optimal?

  • Ensures short-run cost reflectiveness
  • Can create revenue uncertainty and political concerns

Reform Proposals:

  • Contracts for Difference (CfDs): Price stabilization for generators, reducing volatility but potential market distortions
  • Splitting markets: Separating renewable and conventional generation markets
  • Temporary interventions: Gas price caps or windfall taxes (Spain, Italy), capacity mechanisms or industrial electricity prices (debated in Germany)

Market Economy vs. State Intervention:

  • Market prices are essential for innovation and investment
  • Current volatile market prices create new business models (e.g., battery storage through arbitrage)
  • State interventions (capacity mechanisms, industrial electricity prices) can distort and dilute price signals
  • Some interventions are politically necessary to ensure acceptance and supply security
  • Balance needed between efficiency and steering

Future Perspective

  • More integration of all actors
  • Technology-neutral and market-economically supported
  • No radical system break planned
  • Balanced adjustments to improve system efficiency and supply security

Mathematical & Economic Modeling

Market Operations

Advanced market management relies on:

  • Optimization algorithms: Unit commitment, economic dispatch, and real-time balancing solved via linear and mixed-integer programming
  • Game theory and strategic bidding: Generators optimize bids under market rules to maximize profits under uncertainty
  • Stochastic forecasting: Managing weather-dependent renewables with probabilistic models

Future Outlook

Skills for Energy Professionals

Professionals must:

  • Understand evolving market designs and regulatory frameworks internationally
  • Analyze market impacts of technology shifts (storage, demand-side response, hydrogen, digitalization)
  • Use quantitative tools for market and risk analysis
  • Engage in policy dialogues to shape designs balancing sustainability, reliability, and affordability

Key Challenges

  • Reconciling efficiency with investment signals
  • Balancing fairness and consumer protection
  • Integrating massive renewable capacity
  • Managing geopolitical shocks and market volatility

Key Takeaways

The electricity market is a complex, layered system balancing instantaneous supply and demand.

Core Principles:

  1. Marginal pricing grounded in merit order dispatch drives operational efficiency
  2. Market layers (forward, day-ahead, intraday, balancing) work together
  3. Renewable integration presents challenges requiring flexibility solutions
  4. Market reforms aim to reconcile efficiency, investment signals, fairness, and consumer protection

Discussion Questions

Critical Questions for Debate

  1. Is marginal pricing still optimal?
    • Does it ensure efficiency while providing adequate investment signals?
    • How should we handle windfall profits during price spikes?
  2. Market vs. Regulation Balance
    • How much state intervention is acceptable?
    • Can we maintain market efficiency while ensuring supply security?
  3. Industrial Electricity Price
    • Should energy-intensive industries receive price protection?
    • What are better alternatives to direct price caps?
  4. Capacity Mechanisms
    • Are capacity markets necessary or can market-based solutions work?
    • How to ensure investment in rarely-used but critical capacity?
  5. Digitalization and Flexibility
    • How can digitalization improve market efficiency?
    • What role should flexibility services play in future markets?
  6. Market Segmentation
    • Should renewables and conventional generation be in separate markets?
    • How would this impact investment incentives?
